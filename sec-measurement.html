    Section 3.2 Measurement

    Measurement helps us find out how long, wide, or far things are. We use it every day when building, cooking, or even drawing. In this topic, we will learn how to measure the edges and spaces of different shapes, including how to find the perimeter and area using simple formulas.

    Subsection 3.2.1 Perimeter and length

    \(\textbf{Perimeter}\)
    1. The perimeter of a shape is the total distance around its edges.
    2. To find the perimeter, you add the lengths of all the sides of the shape.
    3. Example:
      For a rectangle with length 6 cm and width 4 cm:
      \begin{align*} \text{Perimeter}= \amp 2 \times (6+4) \\ = \amp 20 \,cm \end{align*}
    \(\textbf{Length}\)
    1. Length usually refers to how long an object is from end to end.
    2. In a rectangle, the longer side is often called the length, while the shorter is called the width.
    3. It’s a one-dimensional measure (just one side), not a total like perimeter.
    Note
    \(\text{Length} = \) one side (e.g., \(6\, cm\))
    \(\text{Perimeter} = \) all sides added together (e.g., \(20 \,cm \)for a rectangle)

    \(\textbf{Key Takeaway}\)
    • The circumference is the distance round a circle.
    • When you divide the circumference by the diameter of any circular object, the number you get is approximately \(3.142\) correct to three decimal places.
      \begin{equation*} \frac{\text{Circumference}}{\text{diameter}} = 3.142 \end{equation*}
      Circumference \(= 3.14 \times\text {diameter}\)
    • The number \(3.142\) is called \(\textbf {pi}\) and is represented by the symbol \(\pi\text{.}\) As a fraction, \(\pi = \frac {22}{7}\text{.}\) Therefore, circumference \(= \pi d\)
      or
      \begin{align*} \pi= \amp \frac{C}{D}=\amp \frac{C}{2r} \end{align*}
    • Two radii are equal to the diameter. Hence \(d = 2r\)
      Circumference
      \begin{align*} = \amp \pi \times d\\ = \amp \pi \times 2r\\ = \amp 2\pi r \end{align*}

    Subsubsection 3.2.2.2 Length of an arc

    An arc of a circle is a portion of the circumference (the curved edge) of the circle.
    There are two main types of arcs:
    1. Minor Arc – less than half the circle (less than \(180^\circ\)).
    2. Major Arc – more than half the circle (more than \(180^\circ\)).

    Activity 3.2.10.

    \(\textbf{Key Takeaway}\)
    A rhombus is a type of quadrilateral that is a special kind of parallelogram. The key characteristics of a rhombus are:
    1. Opposite sides are parallel.
    2. All sides have equal length.
    3. Opposite angles are equa and adjacent angles are supplementary (they add up to \(180°\)).
    4. The diagonals of a rhombus bisect each other at right angles (90°), meaning they intersect at a right angle and divide each other into two equal parts.
    The formula for the area \(A\) of a rhombus can be given in two common ways:
    • Using the diagonals:
      \begin{equation*} A=\frac{1}{2}\times d_1 \times d_2 \end{equation*}
      where:
      \(d_1 \)and \(d_2\) are the lengths of the two diagonals.
    • Using the base and height:
      \begin{equation*} A= \text{base} \times \text{height} \end{equation*}
      Where the base is the length of any side (since all sides are equal) and the height is the perpendicular distance between opposite sides.

    Example 3.2.33.

    \begin{align*} A=\amp \frac{1}{2}d_1 d_2\\ A=\amp \frac{1}{2} (6 \times 8)cm^2 \\ A=\amp 24 cm^2 \end{align*}

    \begin{align*} A= \amp \text{base} \times \text{height}\\ =\amp 5m \times 4m \\ = \amp 20m^2 \end{align*}
    (b)
    \begin{align*} A= \amp \text{base} \times \text{height} \\ =\amp 9cm \times 7cm \\ = \amp 63cm^2 \end{align*}

    Example 3.2.35.

    A rhombus has a diagonal of 10 cm and another diagonal of 24 cm. Find:
    i) The area of the rhombus.
    ii).The length of one side of the rhombus.
    Solution.
    i.
    One diagonal = 10 cm
    Another diagonal = 24 cm
    \begin{align*} \text{Area} = \amp \frac{1}{2 }\times d_1 \times d_2\\ = \amp \frac{1}{2} \times 10\, \text{cm} \times 24\, \text{cm} \\ = \amp 120 \, \text{cm}^2 \end{align*}
    ii. Length of One Side of the Rhombus
    In a rhombus, the diagonals bisect each other at 90°. That means each side of the rhombus forms a right-angled triangle with half of each diagonal.
    Let’s find the length of one side using the Pythagorean Theorem.
    Each side of the rhombus is the hypotenuse of a right triangle with legs:
    \begin{align*} \text{Half of AC } = \amp \frac{10}{2} = 5\, \text{cm}\\ \text{Half of BD } = \amp \frac{24}{2} = 12\, \text{cm}\\ \text{Side} = \amp \sqrt{(5)^2 + (12)^2} \\ = \amp \sqrt{25\,\text{cm} + 144\,\text{cm}} \\ = \amp \sqrt{169 \,\text{cm}}\\ = \amp 13\,\text{cm} \end{align*}

    Example 3.2.39.

    Kisumu and Nanyuki are situated in such a way that Nanyuki is on a bearing of \(075^\circ\) from Nakuru and Kisumu on a bearing of \(280^\circ\) from Nakuru. If Kisumu is \(190\) km and Nanyuki is \(160\) km from Nakuru, find the bearing of Kisumu from Nanyuki.
    Solution.
    First, we draw a sketch showing the positions of Nakuru (N), Nanyuki (A), and Kisumu (B). The angle between the bearings from Nakuru to Nanyuki and Kisumu is:
    \(\angle ANB = 280^\circ - 75^\circ = 205^\circ\)
    Since this angle is greater than 180°, we use the interior angle in triangle ANB:
    \(\angle ANB = 360^\circ - 205^\circ = 155^\circ\)
    Now, apply the cosine rule to find the distance \(AB\) (distance from Nanyuki to Kisumu):
    \(AB^2 = AN^2 + NB^2 - 2 \cdot AN \cdot NB \cdot \cos(\angle ANB)\)
    \(AB^2 = 160^2 + 190^2 - 2 \cdot 160 \cdot 190 \cdot \cos(155^\circ)\)
    \(AB^2 = 25600 + 36100 - 2 \cdot 160 \cdot 190 \cdot \cos(155^\circ)\)
    \(AB^2 ≈ 25600 + 36100 - 60800 \cdot (-0.9063)\)
    \(AB^2 ≈ 61700 + 55103.04 = 116803.04\)
    \(AB ≈ \sqrt{116803.04} ≈ 341.8 \text{ km}\)
    Now, use the sine rule to find the angle at Nanyuki (angle NAB):
    \(\frac{\sin(\angle NAB)}{NB} = \frac{\sin(155^\circ)}{AB}\)
    \(\sin(\angle NAB) = \frac{190 \cdot \sin(155^\circ)}{341.8}\)
    \(\sin(\angle NAB) ≈ \frac{190 \cdot 0.4226}{341.8} ≈ 0.235\)
    \(\angle NAB ≈ \sin^{-1}(0.235) ≈ 13.6^\circ\)
    Finally, the bearing of Kisumu from Nanyuki is:
    \(075^\circ + 13.6^\circ = 088.6^\circ\)
    Answers:
    1. The bearing of Kisumu from Nanyuki is approximately \(089^\circ\text{.}\)
    2. The distance from Nanyuki to Kisumu is approximately \(342\) km.

    Subsection 3.2.11 Vectors

    In mathematics, vectors are a fundamental concept that goes beyond numbers. Suppose I ask, "How far is your home from school?" One possible response is " \(2\) kilometers."
