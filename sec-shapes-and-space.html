    2. Mark a point \(P\) outside the line as shown below.
    3. With the compass point on \(P\text{,}\) draw an arc that cuts the line \(AB\) and label them as \(C\) and \(D\) as shown below.
    4. (a).Place the compass on point \(C\) and draw an arc below and above the line.

    What you need: A ruler, A compass and A pencil.

    2. With the compass centered at point A and radius equal to the length of segment AB, draw two arcs—one above and one below the line segment AB as shown below
    3. Without changing the compass width, place the compass at point B and draw arcs above and below the line to intersect the arcs drawn from point A as shown below.
    4. Mark the points where the arcs intersect above and below the line segment, and label them as \(P\) and \(Q\) respectively.
    5. Draw a straight line through points P and Q as shown below.

    Subsection 3.1.7 Bisecting of Angles

    Activity 3.1.4.

    \(\textbf{ How to construct an angle bisector?}\)
    \(\textbf{What you need:}\)A ruler and A compass.
    Given a known or unknown \(\angle PQR\text{,}\) the steps to construct its angle bisector are:
    1. Place the compass pointer at \(Q\) and make an arc that cuts the two arms of the angle at two different points.
    2. From the point where the first arc cut the arm \(QP\text{,}\) make another arc towards the interior of the angle.
    3. Without changing the radius on the compass, repeat step \(2\) from the point where the first arc cut \(QR\text{.}\)
    4. Using a ruler, draw a line from \(Q\) to the point where the arcs intersect.
    \(\textbf{Key Takeaway}\)
    • The line that was drawn through \(Q\) represents the angle bisector of the \(\angle PQR\text{.}\)
    • An angle bisector or the bisector of an angle is a line that divides an angle into two equal parts. For example, In the applet below, the blue line is said to be an angle bisector of angle \(BAC\text{.}\)
    • The gray slider adjusts the entire measure of angle \(BAC\) and the black slider dynamically illustrates what it means for a line to bisect an angle.
    If an angle bisector bisects a line segment at \(90^\circ\text{,}\) it is known as the perpendicular bisector of that line.

    Example 3.1.3.

    Example 3.1.5.

    If an angle bisector divides an angle of \(120\) degrees, then what is the measure of each angle?
    Solution.
    Given, a measure of an angle is \(120^\circ\)
    As we know, the angle bisector divides the angle into equal two parts.
    Therefore, \(120^\circ\) is divided into equal two parts, say \(x\text{.}\)
    Hence,
    \begin{equation*} x^\circ + x^\circ = 120^\circ \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 2x = 120^\circ \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x = \frac{120^\circ}{2} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} x=60^\circ \end{equation*}

    Subsection 3.1.11 Constructing \(\angle 120^\circ\)

    Activity 3.1.9.

    \(\textbf{Work in pairs }\)
    \(\textbf{What you need:}\) A ruler, a protractor and a pair of compasses.
    1.Draw a line and mark a point \(O\) on it.
    2.With \(O\) as center draw an arc of any radius to cut the line at \(A\text{.}\)

    \begin{equation*} \angle AOC = 120^\circ\text{.} \end{equation*}
